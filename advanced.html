A1.Combinatorics and blockers

Everything a solver does starts with counting combos. An unpaired hand has 16 combos (4 suited + 12 offsuit), a pair has 6. Board and hole cards remove combos — and that removal, not intuition, is what decides which hands bluff, which call, and which fold.

  • Bluff selection by blockers: A5s is the classic 3-bet bluff not because it's pretty, but because the ace blocks a third of the continuing range's strongest region (AA, AK, AQ). Fewer continues → more folds → cheaper bluff.
  • Value blocking (the bad kind): holding KQ on K72 you block KK — good — but when the money goes in on K72-K, your second king blocks exactly the hands you beat that pay you (KJ, KT).
  • Unblocking bluffs when you call: the best river bluff-catchers don't block the busted draws villain bluffs with. Holding Q♦J♦ on a board where the ♦ draw missed is a terrible call — you hold their bluffs in your own hand.
Pro benchmark: in any spot you can say within 2 seconds how many combos of the top of villain's range exist given your cards and the board — "he has 7 value combos and I beat 2 of them" is the level of resolution real decisions are made at.

A2.Range morphology: linear, condensed, polarized

Ranges come in three shapes, and recognizing which shape a bet represents is the fastest read in poker. These two grids are live from the trainer's charts — the BB's response to a BTN open, split into its two personalities:

BB 3-bet range — polarized: premiums + blocker bluffs
BB calling range — condensed: strong-but-not-premium
  • Linear (top-down: AA→A9s with no gaps) — opening ranges, value-heavy 3-bets vs weak players. Bet when you're simply "strong".
  • Polarized (nuts + air, nothing in between) — river bets, light 3-bets. Powerful because the opponent's medium hands face an impossible guess.
  • Condensed / capped (all medium, no nuts) — every calling range. This is the exploitable shape: a range that just calls is capped at its best possible holding.

Attacking caps is a core pro skill: when the BB just calls preflop and just calls the flop, they almost never hold AA/KK/sets by the turn — big turn and river barrels print against capped ranges, especially on cards that favor your uncapped range (aces, kings, board pairs).

Pro benchmark: after any two actions you can name the shape of both ranges ("my range is uncapped here, his is condensed") and adjust sizing to it without looking anything up.

A3.Bet sizing theory and geometric growth

Sizing isn't a style choice — each size carries exact math. Two numbers govern all of it, for a bet b into pot P:

α (alpha) = b ÷ (b + P) — how often a pure bluff must work to break even.
Bluff share = b ÷ (P + 2b) — the fraction of a balanced river betting range that is bluffs (equal to the pot odds you offer).

Geometric sizing answers "what size gets stacks in by the river?" — betting the same pot-fraction e on every street such that the last bet is exactly all-in. Computed live for the trainer's standard single-raised pot:

Why it matters: the geometric line maximizes the amount a worse hand can be charged across three streets. When you hold the nuts on the flop and want maximum value, geometric is the default; deviate smaller early only when you want their whole range to continue.

  • Small (25–40%): range bets on boards you dominate — taxing their entire range cheaply.
  • Large (66–100%): polar — value that wants a big pot plus the bluffs that support it.
  • Overbet (125%+): reserved for nut advantage — when only your range holds the top hands (their cap, your uncapped). The bluffs alongside must block their calls.
Pro benchmark: you can quote α and the bluff share for ⅓, ½, ¾, pot and 1.5x without thinking, and you can compute the geometric size for any SPR in your head to within 10%.

That compounding drives real strategy:

  • Plan three streets at the flop. Before calling, ask: "which turns do I continue on?" If the honest answer is "almost none", folding the flop saves two more bets — calling flop to fold every turn is the most expensive habit in poker.
  • Defend with the right composition: your continues need a mix of made hands and draws/backdoors that can become barrels themselves — a calling range of pure bluff-catchers gets ground down street by street.
  • The barreler's mirror: your flop bluffs should mostly hold equity to keep barreling (draws, overcards, backdoors). Pure air wants to bluff once or not at all.
Pro benchmark: you never call a flop bet without already knowing your turn plan on the three main card classes (blank, overcard, draw-completer).

A5.Advanced ICM: risk premium and bubble factors

The fundamentals guide told you ICM tightens calls. The pro version is quantitative: every confrontation has a risk premium — extra equity you need beyond chip-EV pot odds, because tournament chips lost hurt more than chips won help.   • Medium stacks suffer the most. The 30 bb stack needs the biggest edge to fight the 40 bb stack — they have the most ladder equity to lose. The 10 bb stack plays closest to chip-EV: it has little left to protect.
  • Coverage is leverage. The chip leader's risk premium against mediums is small while theirs is huge — that asymmetry, not card advantage, is why big stacks attack relentlessly on bubbles.
  • Laddering is a real strategy: with a huge pay jump ahead, folding 60%-equity spots can be right. Compute it, don't feel it — the required-equity numbers above are the honest bar.
Pro benchmark: before any big all-in near the money you can estimate your risk premium within ~3% — "this is a 50/50 in chips but I need 58% here" — and fold hands that feel unfoldable.

A6.Multiway pots: why everything tightens

Almost all solver material is heads-up, but tournament pots go multiway constantly — and equity dilutes fast. Live Monte Carlo from this page's evaluator, versus random hands:

  • Bluffing collapses. A bluff must fold out everyone. If two players each defend correctly against your size, your bluff succeeds far less often than α requires — the solver's answer is to barely bluff multiway. Value-bet relentlessly instead.
  • Value thresholds rise. Top pair good kicker is a three-street value hand heads-up and often a one-street hand three-way. "Someone has it" is real math, not fear.
  • Position compounds: being last of three is worth more than last of two — everyone checks to the raiser, and you realize equity free.
  • Preflop consequence: with callers likely behind (weak players who never fold), tighten your opens toward hands that flop strong pairs and nut draws, and stop light-3-betting into fields that call everything.
Pro benchmark: in a 3-way pot your c-bet frequency drops by roughly half on most textures, and you can articulate why for the specific board.

A7.Exploitative play: deviating on purpose

GTO is the baseline; money comes from measured deviation. The math of exploiting is just alpha in reverse — computed live for a ½-pot bluff (α = 33%):

  • Population reads that survive across pools: low/mid-stakes fields under-bluff big rivers (overfold vs their jams), over-fold to turn and river check-raises (raise more value AND more bluffs), over-call flops and give up turns (double-barrel more), and almost never 4-bet bluff (fold to 4-bets).
  • Minimum exploitative deviation: shift frequency in the profitable direction without inventing new actions. If they overfold, bluff more of your natural bluff candidates — don't start bluffing hands with showdown value.
  • The counter-exploit check: every deviation opens a door. Bluffing every river vs an overfolder is printing until they call once and you realize they've adjusted — date your reads, and re-anchor to baseline against anyone competent or unknown.
  • Node-locking mentality: when you study, don't just read the GTO chart — ask "and if villain folds this node 10% too often, what becomes a bet?" That question is where win-rate lives.
Pro benchmark: for each regular in your pool you hold 2–3 specific, dated deviations — and you can state what evidence would make you revert each one.

A8.Short-stack weapons beyond push/fold

The fundamentals cover jam-or-fold. Pros add three tools around it:

  • The resteal, quantified. Reshoving 18 bb over a 2.2x CO open risks 18 bb to win ~5.2 bb (blinds + antes + the open). Breakeven fold equity with zero pot equity would be ~78% — but you always have equity when called, which is why the solver reshoves A5s (blockers + live cards) long before A9o (dominated when called). Fold equity plus blockers, never "I had an ace".
  • Stop-and-go: at 8–12 bb in the blinds, flat the open and jam any flop instead of jamming pre — villain must hit the board to call, so you fold out equity a preflop jam never could. Best with hands that hate being called pre (small pairs, suited connectors).
  • The min-raise-fold at 12–16 bb: pure jam-or-fold surrenders the cheap steal. Solvers mix small opens even at these depths from late position, folding to jams with the bottom — it works because your jamming range still exists to protect it. Never min-raise-fold a hand that plays fine as a jam.
  • Limping (beyond this trainer's model): at ≤12 bb the SB develops limp ranges in real solves — completing gives the BB a chance to see flops cheap with your whole range protected by limp-jams. Our tree doesn't model limps; know that the pro baseline includes them and study SB limp charts separately.
Pro benchmark: between 8 and 20 bb you never feel "card dead" — every orbit you can name which seat you'd attack with which tool, and your reshove ranges shift correctly with the opener's position.

A9.Turn play: equity shifts and barrel selection

The turn is where c-bet autopilot dies. Which card falls changes whose range the board favors — computed live, BTN's opening range vs BB's defend on K♠7♦2♣ as five different turns land:

  • Barrel range cards, check-back caller cards. Aces and broadways extend your advantage — barrel wide. Middling connectors (8s, 6s, 5s) hit the BB's defend range — slow down with your air.
  • Barrel selection within the range: continue with value that grew, draws that grew (added equity), and bluffs holding blockers to their continues. Give up pure air that gained nothing.
  • Delayed c-bet: checking back the flop then betting most turns is a real line, not a failure — it realizes equity with medium hands and attacks the BB's "he gave up" probes.
  • Facing probes: when the flop checks through, the BB leads many turns; your check-back range must continue with all pairs and good aces or you're folding the best hand constantly.
Pro benchmark: shown any flop, you can sort the 47 turn cards into "barrel / mixed / give up" classes in under 30 seconds — and the live table above lets you check yourself.

A10.River mastery: polarization endpoints

The river is pure math wearing a poker face. A balanced betting range holds bluffs in exact proportion to the price offered (the A3 table); everything else is hand-reading through blockers:

  • Value first: list villain's calling range; if it contains more combos you beat than beat you, bet. "Thin value" is just doing this honestly with second pair. Checking back strong-but-not-nutted on scary rivers is discipline, not weakness — being check-raised off the best hand is the true cost.
  • Bluff candidates rank by blockers: the best river bluff blocks the calls (their strongest bluff-catchers, e.g. holding the missed nut-flush ace on a paired river) and unblocks the folds. The worst "bluff" is a hand with showdown value — that's burning a free bluff-catcher.
  • Calling is combo accounting: against a pot-size jam you need 33%. Count their plausible value combos given your blockers, apply the bluff share their sizing should carry, then adjust for the population read (most under-bluff). The trainer's river drill states its model explicitly — pros do the same silently.
  • Raise-size tells at equilibrium don't exist — but in fields they do: small river raises are almost pure value everywhere below high stakes. Fold your bluff-catchers.
Pro benchmark: facing a river jam you can narrate: "value combos I lose to: N; combos I beat that jam: M; bluffs needed for a call at this price: X — call/fold" in under a minute.

A11.Solver literacy: reading mixes and simplifying

Owning solver output means knowing what it does and doesn't say:

  • Mixes are indifference, not indecision. "Bet 30% / check 70%" means both actions have equal EV for that combo — the frequency exists to keep the opponent's counter-strategy from profiting. At the table you may pure-play either side of a mix at almost zero cost; what you may not do is pick the third option the solver never uses.
  • EV loss is the only real currency. A "mistake" costing 0.03 bb is noise; the trainer's thresholds (~0.05–0.1 bb) reflect that. Chase the 1 bb+ errors in your recap, ignore frequency-nitpicks.
  • Simplify like the pros: one bet size per node (chosen by texture), mixes rounded to 0/50/100, and whole-range strategies ("range-bet this texture class") — measured EV loss of good simplifications is tiny, and execution improves more than equilibrium purity is lost.
  • Study aggregates, not hands: the pattern "c-bet 70%+ small on A-high dry, 35% polar on low-connected" transfers; a single solved hand doesn't. The texture quiz and explorer are aggregate tools — use them that way.
  • Know your model. This trainer solves single streets with equity rollouts, two bet sizes, one raise size, heads-up. That's ideal for fundamentals and honest about what it isn't. When a line feels off in a deeper tree, that's your cue to check a multi-street solver — not to distrust the concept.
Pro benchmark: you can look at any strategy grid in this app and explain why each region mixes — which indifference is being maintained and against what counter-strategy.

A12.Variance, bankroll, and actually going pro

The final skill is surviving your own results. MTT variance is brutal in ways cash players never face — this simulation runs live on this page: 2,000 careers of 500 tournaments each, played by an identical winning player (~25% ROI, top-heavy payouts):

  • Read that table again. A genuinely good player loses money over 500-tournament stretches a meaningful fraction of the time. Results tell you almost nothing on samples under a thousand games — EV-loss numbers (like this trainer's) are the only same-day feedback that's real.
  • Bankroll: 100 average buy-ins is a floor for serious online MTT play; 200–300+ for a pro with expenses. Shot-take upward with 5–10 buy-ins earmarked, drop back down without ego.
  • Volume and game selection beat hero reads: ROI is made by table softness and consistency, not occasional brilliance. The softest field you can beat at your roll is the right field.
  • The semi-pro path: keep income stable, put in 15–25 focused hours a week (⅓ study, ⅔ play), review every session's mistakes (the Review button is exactly this), and judge yourself on decision quality per month, not cashes per week.
Pro benchmark: you can state your ROI confidence interval honestly, your downswing tolerance in buy-ins, and your stop-loss/shot-take rules — written down before you need them.
