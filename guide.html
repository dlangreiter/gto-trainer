1.What "GTO" actually means

Game Theory Optimal play is a strategy that cannot be exploited: if you played it forever, no opponent could adjust in any way that beats you. It is not the most profitable strategy against bad players — against someone who calls too much you should simply bluff less and value-bet more — but it is the baseline. You cannot deviate profitably from a baseline you don't know.

Two ideas underpin everything that follows:

  • Ranges, not hands. You never play "a hand" — you play every hand you could hold the same way you would in that spot. The solver's answer is always a frequency for each of the 169 starting hands: "jam 100%", "call 40%", "fold". Mixed frequencies exist to stay unexploitable.
  • EV (expected value) is the average chips a decision earns over all outcomes. The trainer grades you by EV lost: how many big blinds your choice gives up versus the best option. A "mistake" that loses 0.05 bb is noise; one that loses 2 bb is a leak.
The goal of studying GTO is not to memorize charts — it's to internalize why the ranges look the way they do, so you can reconstruct them at the table and know when (and how) to deviate against real opponents.

2.Positions and the table

Position is the single biggest input to every preflop decision. Acting later means acting with more information, so late positions play far more hands. At a 9-handed table the seats act in this order before the flop:

Preflop action starts at UTG and ends with the Big Blind. The Button acts last on every postflop street — the most valuable seat at the table.
  • Early (UTG, UTG+1, UTG+2) — eight players still to act behind you: open only strong hands.
  • Middle (LJ, HJ) — ranges widen as fewer players remain.
  • Late (CO, BTN) — the button opens roughly four times as many hands as UTG.
  • Blinds (SB, BB) — forced bets. The BB closes the preflop action and gets a discount to defend; the SB plays the whole game out of position.

3.Reading the 169-hand grid

Every chart in poker uses the same 13×13 grid. Pairs run down the diagonal. Hands above the diagonal are suited (both cards the same suit — they make flushes and play better), hands below it are offsuit. "AKs" = ace-king suited (4 combos), "AKo" = offsuit (12 combos), "AA" = 6 combos. There are 1,326 total two-card combos compressed into these 169 classes.

Diagonal = pairs · upper-right = suited · lower-left = offsuit.

When a chart shows a colored cell, the color's height is the frequency: a cell half-filled red jams 50% of the time. Mixed strategies are normal — memorize the pure regions first, treat the mixes as "either is fine".

5.Defending against a raise: 3-bet, call, or fold

Facing an open you have three options, and the right mix depends on your position relative to the opener and the price you're getting:

  • 3-bet a polarized-to-linear mix: your best hands for value, plus hands slightly too weak to call that block their continues (A5s, K9s type hands).
  • Call mostly in the BB (you already have 1 bb invested — you're getting a discount) and in position with hands that flop well: pairs, suited broadways, suited connectors.
  • Fold everything else. The single most common leak in real games is defending too many dominated offsuit hands.

The BB defends far wider than any other seat — closing the action at a discount, roughly 25–40% of hands against late opens. The SB, out of position with no discount, mostly plays 3-bet-or-fold.

6.Pot odds and equity — the only math you must know

Equity is your share of the pot if all cards were dealt out now — your winning chances. Pot odds tell you the equity you need for a call to break even:

Required equity = amount to call ÷ (pot after you call).
Facing a 5 bb bet into a 10 bb pot: 5 ÷ (10 + 5 + 5) = 25%. Your call only needs to win a quarter of the time.
The bigger the bet relative to the pot, the more equity your call needs — but it never reaches 50% because the pot subsidizes you.

Common landmarks worth memorizing: ⅓-pot bet → 20%, ½-pot → 25%, ¾-pot → 30%, pot-size → 33%, 2x pot → 40%. Every calling decision in this trainer — preflop all-ins and river jams alike — reduces to "is my equity above the requirement?"

7.Short stacks: push or fold

Below roughly 15 bb, raising small and folding to a shove burns too much of your stack, so the solved strategy collapses to two actions: all-in or fold. Jamming works through two channels at once — everyone folds and you win the blinds/antes uncontested, or you get called with decent equity. That "fold equity" is why hands like K7s or A2o become clear jams at 8 bb even though they're behind most calling ranges.

  • Ranges widen dramatically by position: at 10 bb with an ante, UTG jams ~15% but the SB jams over half of all hands.
  • Ranges widen as stacks shrink: the shorter you are, the more the blinds matter relative to your stack — and the less you risk when called.
  • Known benchmark: heads-up at 10 bb the SB jams ~58% of hands (the trainer's solver reproduces this to within 0.1%).

8.Calling all-ins

Calling a jam is pure pot-odds math against the jammer's range, not their exact hand. A 10 bb jam into 2.5 bb of blinds and antes offers you roughly 45% required equity — so you need hands that beat the middle of their jamming range. Two asymmetries trip people up:

  • Calling ranges are always tighter than jamming ranges — the jammer has fold equity, the caller has none. Never mirror the jam range when calling.
  • The wider the jammer's range (late position, short stack), the wider you call. A5o is a snap call against a SB 10bb jam and a clear fold against a UTG jam.

9.Antes change everything

Once antes are in play (usually from level 3 of a tournament), every pot starts with ~2.5 bb instead of 1.5 bb. The reward for winning preflop rises ~65% while the risk stays the same, so every range widens: opens, jams, calls, defends. The trainer's solver shows UTG's 10 bb jam range growing from ~10% without an ante to ~15% with one. If you play ante-less ranges in ante levels you are folding away equity every orbit.

10.Mid stacks (15–30 bb): the raise tree

Between 15 and 30 bb you have three first-in options: fold, small raise (2–2.5x), or jam. The solved trees show a clear structure:

  • Open small with almost everything you play. Pure open-jams are rare above 15 bb — the small raise wins the blinds nearly as often at a fraction of the risk, and keeps your options open.
  • Reshoving is the counter: facing a 2.2x open with 15–25 bb, jamming denies the opener's equity and gets called by a tight range. Reshove ranges are value + blockers (Ax suited shines).
  • Facing a reshove after you open is pure pot-odds again: you're usually offered ~35–40%, so you call with far more hands than feels natural. Open-folding AJo after a reshove is often a big mistake — the trainer will show you the exact EV.

11.ICM: chips are not money

In a tournament, doubling your stack does not double your prize-money expectation — payouts flatten the top. The Independent Chip Model converts stacks into tournament equity, and it changes solved ranges in one systematic direction: survival gains value.

  • On the bubble, a big stack can jam recklessly wide into medium stacks (they can't call without risking their tournament), while calling ranges collapse — in the trainer's classic bubble example the BTN's jam range jumps from 22% to 53% and the SB's calling range falls from 12% to 1.5%.
  • Close to par (equal stacks, far from payouts) ICM barely matters. The deeper into the money, the stronger the effect.
  • The practical rule: jam wider when you cover, call tighter when calling risks your stack.

12.PKO bounties

In progressive-knockout tournaments, part of the prize pool sits on players' heads. A bounty is dead money you can only claim by covering and busting someone — so it works like extra chips in the pot that only you can win. Convert the bounty to big blinds (bounty $ ÷ $ per bb) and the effects follow mechanically:

  • Calling ranges vs shorter stacks explode — the trainer's property tests show a 5 bb jam that a 41% range calls normally becomes a call with any two cards when the jammer carries a big bounty.
  • Your own jams get called wider when you're short and bountied — tighten your bluff jams.
  • If you don't cover the player, their bounty changes nothing for you. Coverage is everything.

13.Postflop I: equity, ranges, and MDF

Postflop is the same game with more information: your preflop range meets a board. Three tools carry most of the weight:

  • Hand-vs-range equity. Stop thinking "am I ahead?" and start thinking "what's my equity against everything they can have?" Top pair might be 80% against a wide range and 35% against a tight one.
  • Range advantage. Whichever player's range connects better with the board gets to bet more — section 14.
  • Minimum Defense Frequency. When you face a bet, MDF = pot ÷ (pot + bet) is the fraction of your range you must continue with so that their pure bluffs can't print money. Vs a ⅓-pot bet defend ~75%; vs a pot-size bet defend ~50%. Folding much more than MDF against frequent bettors is the most exploitable postflop leak.

The pattern generalizes:

  • Low, connected boards (654, 765tt) favor the caller — the BB defends every suited connector and small pair that just smashed this flop. The raiser should check more and give up more.
  • Middling boards (T98, J76tt) are contested — betting gets polar: strong hands and draws bet bigger, the middle checks.

15.Postflop III: c-betting and defending

The continuation bet is the most common postflop decision in poker. The solver's c-bet strategy follows from range advantage plus your specific hand's needs:

  • Small bets (⅓ pot) on boards you dominate — a cheap attack the defender must respect with ~75% of range (MDF), which is impossible on boards that missed them.
  • Big bets (¾ pot+) when ranges are closer and you want to play polar: strong value + draws, and the middle of your range checks.
  • Checking is not giving up — medium-strength hands check to control the pot and bluff-catch.
  • Defending, raise the c-bet with your strongest hands plus draws — check-raising only value is face-up and exploitable.

After any c-bet or defend drill, hit "View solver range" to see the whole strategy grid for that exact board — the fastest way to build intuition for how ranges split.

16.Postflop IV: turns, rivers, and polarization

As streets progress, ranges narrow and betting turns polar: by the river, big bets represent either strong value or a bluff — medium hands check or bluff-catch. Three river principles:

  • Count the price, not your feelings. A pot-size river jam needs you to win 33% of the time. Against a range of "top 20% value + 12% bluffs", a mediocre bluff-catcher often clears that bar.
  • Value bets need calls from worse; bluffs need folds from better. If neither is true, check.
  • Balanced barreling: the solver keeps enough bluffs (usually busted draws with blockers) that folding everything but the nuts loses; the trainer's river-call drill trains exactly this judgment.
